Final Project Reflection

Learning to Lead From the Inside Out

An MIS student's journey through leadership, self-awareness, creativity, and the soft skills that separate good technologists from great ones.

I Introduction

What This Course Asked of Me

As an MIS student, I came into this course confident in a specific kind of competence: solving technical problems, building systems, and delivering results. Leadership, in my mind, was something that happened after you proved your technical ability. INTB 3355 challenged that framing immediately.

Through readings from the Harvard Business Review, the Keirsey Temperament Sorter, and structured reflection, I came to understand that technical skill alone is a ceiling. Real leadership begins with self-knowledge and expresses itself through how we listen to, question, and develop the people around us.

II Starting Point

Where I Began

Before the semester started, I would have described myself as technically sharp, detail-oriented, and reliable under pressure. In group projects I gravitated toward execution building the spreadsheet, setting up the system, coding the prototype. My teammates appreciated the reliability.

What I lacked was depth in the human side of leadership. I preferred arriving with answers over asking open questions. My feedback to peers was consistently vague I equated directness with conflict. I assumed creativity belonged to designers, not someone who spent evenings writing SQL queries. And despite knowing mentors mattered, I had never deliberately pursued one.

III Learning

What the Course Taught Me

Four HBR readings and one personality assessment formed the backbone of my learning. Together they told a unified story: that great leaders are curious, attentive, and humble not the loudest voice in the room.

Finkelstein HBR

What Amazing Bosses Do Differently

The best leaders manage people as individuals, not interchangeable team members. They provide ongoing feedback, listen more than they speak, and stay consistent. The common denominator is attentiveness.

Hagel HBR

Good Leadership Is About Asking Good Questions

Providing answers does not build trust expressing thoughtful uncertainty and inviting collaboration does. Open questions create cultures of learning and spark the innovative thinking organizations need.

Satell HBR

Set the Conditions for Anyone to Be Creative

Creativity is not a personality trait it is a product of environment. Leaders who cultivate expertise, encourage exploration, and reward persistence make breakthrough thinking possible for anyone.

Horoszowski HBR

How to Build a Great Relationship with a Mentor

Mentorship requires defined goals, a deliberate ask, and structured accountability. People with mentors advance faster yet more than half of professionals lack one.

The Keirsey Temperament Sorter placed me as an ESTJ a Guardian type characterized by structure and decisiveness. Reading the portrait was humbling: I recognized my strengths but also my blind spots impatience with ambiguity and a tendency to prioritize efficiency over exploration. It reframed colleagues with contrasting styles as bringing perspectives my instincts were likely suppressing.

IV Application

Putting It Into Practice

Asking Better Questions Systems Analysis Project

I was accustomed to opening team meetings with a task list. After reading Hagel, I tried asking instead: "What's the one thing slowing us down that we haven't talked about?" A teammate admitted he had been building on a flawed data model for two weeks but hadn't wanted to raise it. That one question caught a structural error before we built three more layers on top of it.

Managing Individuals Apple Product Specialist

As a Product Specialist, I ran the same walkthrough for every customer. After reading Finkelstein, I started each interaction by asking about their specific situation first. Adapting my approach to each person rather than running a script directly contributed to the 96% NPS I maintained, and changed how I handed off issues to the technical team.

Building a Mentorship American EMR Network

Using Horoszowski's framework, I wrote out what I actually needed in a mentor for the first time: someone who had moved from a technical background into a business-facing role. I had previously crossed paths with a senior product leader during my time at American EMR but had never thought to approach them intentionally. Applying Horoszowski's method, I framed the ask as one focused conversation with no open-ended commitment. Having that clarity made the outreach feel natural rather than awkward, and it is a skill I will carry into every professional relationship going forward.

Protecting Ideas META Case Competition

The META Case Competition took place before this course, but reflecting on it through Satell's framework was clarifying. When a teammate proposed an unconventional tiered workforce model, the group's instinct was to dismiss it as too complex. I had pushed back on that instinct and asked the group to explore it first. At the time I could not articulate why. Satell gave me the language: protecting "ugly babies" from premature judgment. That idea became the backbone of our proposal and the framework the judges singled out for its originality. Understanding the principle behind what I did intuitively means I can now apply it deliberately.

V New Skills & Growth

Who I Am Becoming

I arrived believing leadership was a soft complement to hard technical skill. I leave understanding it as the multiplier that determines whether technical skill creates value or stays siloed. An MIS professional who cannot ask the right questions, develop the people around them, or create space for creativity will always be limited by what they can build alone.

The skills I have genuinely developed this semester: leading through open questions rather than asserting answers; treating teammates and customers as individuals rather than roles; creating conditions for creative thinking rather than suppressing it; and pursuing mentorship with intention rather than waiting for it to appear.

I am aiming toward roles in product management, business analysis, or technology consulting fields where translating between technical and human realities is the core competency. I plan to deepen these skills through continued mentorship, deliberate practice in team settings, and one personal rule I have already started keeping: ask one genuine open question before offering any answer in a professional context.

VI Conclusion

A Changed Orientation

"Expressing vulnerability and asking for help is a strong signal to others that you are trusting and you're more likely to be trusted in return." - John Hagel III

INTB 3355 gave me a framework for growth that is both intellectually grounded and personally honest. I came in with real strengths systems thinking, analytical precision, a bias toward execution but I was operating on an incomplete theory of what leadership requires of a technically-oriented professional.

The readings were not abstract they were mirrors. Each reflected something I recognized from my own experience at Apple, American EMR, Lone Star College, and in case competitions. The Keirsey assessment named tendencies I had always felt but never examined, and gave me vocabulary to understand the people around me with more generosity.

As I move into my final year at Bauer, I carry this semester not as a checklist of skills but as a changed orientation. Leadership is not a destination it is a daily practice, and this course gave me both the motivation and the tools to take it seriously.
